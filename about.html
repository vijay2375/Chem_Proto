About Ink

Understanding the history, science, and importance of ink

📜

History & Background

Ink has been used for writing and documentation for over 5,000 years. Early civilizations such as the Egyptians and Chinese developed inks using natural pigments, soot, and plant extracts.

Iron gall ink became the standard writing ink in Europe from the 12th to the 19th century and was widely used in official records, manuscripts, and artwork.

🧴

What is Iron Gall Ink?

Iron gall ink is a dark purple-black ink made from a chemical reaction between iron salts (ferrous sulfate) and tannic acids obtained from plants such as oak galls.

Famous historical figures like Leonardo da Vinci, Johann Sebastian Bach, and Galileo used iron gall ink in their writings and drawings.

⚗️

The Science Behind It

When tannic acid reacts with ferrous sulfate, a complex chemical compound is formed. This compound oxidizes over time, producing a deep, permanent black color.

Gum arabic is added as a binder to improve ink flow and adhesion to paper, while water acts as the solvent.

🖋️ Traditional Uses

Manuscripts, calligraphy, official documents

🎨 Modern Uses

Art, restoration of old documents, research

🧪 Educational Value

Chemistry experiments and historical studies

Why Study Ink Preparation?

Studying ink preparation helps us understand chemical reactions, material science, and the preservation of historical documents. It also demonstrates the practical application of chemistry in daily life.
